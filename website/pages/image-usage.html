Image Usage Online

PROTECT YOUR COMPUTER

Understand the risks and best practices for sharing and using images online to protect your privacy and security.

How Should Everyday Users Think About Image Usage Online?

1. Common User Attitudes and Mistakes with Image Usage

Most people don’t consider image security as important as passwords, but common mistakes include:

Posting personal images publicly on social media without privacy settings.

Using the same profile photo across many platforms, making it easy for scammers to find and impersonate you.

Sharing images that reveal sensitive information (house address, ID cards, travel plans, children’s school, etc.).

Uploading images to random or unsafe sites, which might misuse or resell them.

Downloading images or attachments from unknown sources that may contain malware.

Example: Emma uploads a vacation photo to Instagram, showing her house number and street. She also uses the same selfie as her profile pic on Facebook, Twitter, and LinkedIn.

2. Risks and Consequences of Poor Image Usage

Identity Theft and Impersonation: Scammers can use your images to create fake profiles and trick your friends or family.

Doxxing: Publicly sharing or leaking personal images can reveal your location, workplace, or private details.

Phishing and Malware: Downloading or clicking images/attachments from unknown emails or messages can infect your device.

Reputation Damage: Embarrassing or inappropriate images can be copied, shared, or manipulated out of your control.

Social Engineering: Criminals can learn about your life, habits, or family from images and use that to guess passwords or answer security questions.

Example: Emma’s house is burglarized while she’s away because her travel photos revealed she wasn’t home, and her street address was visible.

3. Practical Steps to Improve Image Usage Security

a) Use Privacy Settings

Always set social media profiles and albums to private or friends-only if possible.

Review who can see your posts and images.

b) Be Selective with What You Share

Avoid sharing images that include sensitive information (ID cards, plane tickets, location details, children’s schools, etc.).

Remove or blur personal info in images before posting.

c) Vary Profile Images

Use different profile pictures for different accounts. This makes it harder for attackers to link your online identities.

d) Reverse Image Search

Occasionally use tools like Google Reverse Image Search to see where your photos are appearing online.

e) Think Before You Click or Download

Don’t download images or attachments from suspicious emails or unknown sources—they may contain malware.

Be cautious of links or messages that seem out of character, even if they appear to come from friends.

f) Watermark Important Photos

If you share original work (art, photography), add a visible watermark to discourage theft.

4. Additional Tips for Safe Online Behavior

Spot Suspicious Links and Emails

Be wary of unexpected emails/messages with images or attachments.

Check the sender address and hover over links before clicking.

Keep Devices and Software Updated

Regularly update your phone, computer, browser, and antivirus software to protect against vulnerabilities.

Manage Account Recovery

Use secure recovery emails/phone numbers.

Avoid using information visible in your images as answers to security questions (pet’s name, birthday, school, etc.).

Be Mindful of Metadata

Photos contain hidden data (“EXIF”), such as GPS location, device info, and time taken.

Remove or edit metadata before sharing photos publicly. Most photo editors and smartphones have this option.

5. Easy-to-Remember Rules & Checklist

Golden Rules

Think before you share—Would you want this image seen by strangers?

Don’t overshare—Limit what you post, and who can see it.

Verify sources before clicking on any image or link.

Regularly check your digital footprint—See where your images appear online.

Update your devices to stay protected.

Checklist

Social media images set to private

No sensitive info (location, ID) in public images

Different profile photos for different accounts

Watermarks added to important content

Suspicious messages with images ignored/deleted

Metadata stripped from public images

Devices and software up to date

Real-World Scenario

Alex receives an email claiming to be from a friend, with a photo attached. He opens the attachment, and his computer is infected with ransomware.

How Alex Could Have Been Safer:

Verify with his friend via a separate message before opening the attachment.

Keep antivirus and system software up to date.

Be wary of unexpected or out-of-character messages.
