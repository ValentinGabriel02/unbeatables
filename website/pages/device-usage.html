Secure Device Usage

PROTECT YOUR COMPUTER

Learn best practices for securing your devices and protecting your personal information from unauthorized access.

How Everyday Users Should Think About Device Usage

1. Common User Attitudes and Mistakes Around Device Usage

Most people treat their devices (phones, tablets, laptops, desktops) as “personal” and forget that:

Devices often aren’t updated regularly (“it works, so why change it?”).

Devices may be shared with friends/family without considering security.

Passwords/PINs are reused or set to something simple (like “1234” or “password”).

Devices are left unlocked, unattended, or lent to others.

People don’t think about installing apps from unknown sources.

Antivirus is ignored or uninstalled if it causes minor inconvenience.

Important accounts aren’t logged out after use—especially on shared or public computers.

Example: Emily shares her tablet with her kids, uses a simple swipe pattern, and leaves it unlocked on the kitchen counter. She hasn’t updated her apps in months.

2. Risks and Consequences of Poor Device Practices

Credential stuffing: Stolen passwords from one device can lead to other accounts being hacked.

Data breaches: Unprotected devices can be lost, stolen, or hacked—exposing emails, photos, and sensitive data.

Malware/ransomware: Outdated or poorly secured devices are more likely to be infected.

Identity theft: Cybercriminals can use device access to steal identities or commit fraud.

Phishing: Clicking a link in a text, app, or email can compromise the device and all accounts on it.

Example: Emily’s tablet is stolen at a coffee shop. The thief accesses her email and bank app, resetting passwords and transferring funds.

3. Practical Steps to Improve Device Security

a) Set a Strong Lock Screen

Use a PIN, password, fingerprint, or face recognition—never leave your device unlocked.

Avoid simple patterns or codes (e.g., “1234”, “0000”).

b) Enable Automatic Updates

Keep your operating system, apps, and antivirus up to date—enable auto-update when possible.

Updates patch security holes that hackers exploit.

c) Only Install Apps from Trusted Sources

Use official app stores (Google Play, Apple App Store, Microsoft Store).

Avoid “sideloading” apps or downloading from suspicious websites.

d) Use Device Encryption

Enable full-device encryption if available (most modern phones and computers have this built in).

e) Set Up Find My Device/Remote Wipe

Enable “Find My Device” or equivalent feature.

If lost or stolen, you can locate, lock, or erase your device remotely.

f) Separate User Accounts When Sharing Devices

Create separate accounts for different users, especially on computers/tablets.

g) Don’t Save Passwords on Shared/Public Devices

Avoid browser “save password” prompts on public or shared devices.

Always log out of accounts when finished.

h) Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

For all important apps (email, banking, social media).

i) Use a Password Manager

Don’t reuse passwords. Let a manager help you create and store strong ones.

4. Additional Safe Online Habits

Spot suspicious links: Never tap links in strange texts, emails, or DMs.

Review app permissions: Regularly check which apps can access your camera, microphone, location, and contacts.

Back up your device: Use encrypted cloud or local backups so you can recover data if your device is lost or attacked.

Lock or log out: Always lock your device and log out of sensitive accounts on shared computers.

5. Easy-to-Remember Rules & Checklist

Golden Rules

Lock it, update it, back it up!

Only install apps you trust.

Don’t share devices without separate accounts.

Never ignore security pop-ups or updates.

Be cautious with links and downloads.

Device Security Checklist

Strong device lock (PIN/password/biometric)

Device and apps updated automatically

Only official app stores used

Find My Device/remote wipe enabled

Two-factor authentication enabled for important accounts

Regular backups made

App permissions reviewed and limited

Log out on shared devices

Real-World Scenario

Tom lends his unlocked laptop to a neighbor to “check email.” The neighbor installs a free game from a shady website that actually contains malware. Tom later finds out his files are locked and ransom is demanded, and his saved passwords have been stolen.

How Tom Could Have Been Safer:

Require a password or separate user account for guests.

Never allow software to be installed without his approval.

Keep security software and system updates enabled.

Regularly back up important files.
