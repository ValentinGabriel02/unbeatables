Email Security

PROTECT YOUR COMPUTER

Discover how to identify and avoid phishing attempts and other email-based threats to keep your information safe.

How Everyday Users Should Think About Email

1. Common User Attitudes and Mistakes Around Email

Many users:

Treat email addresses as “forever” and use the same one everywhere.

Reuse the same email/password combo on multiple sites.

Use simple or guessable passwords for email accounts.

Share their email publicly (social media profiles, forums, business cards).

Rarely update passwords or security settings.

Open every attachment or link in their inbox without caution.

Ignore security warnings, or think “it won’t happen to me.”

Example: Mike uses the same password for his email and online shopping. He posts his email on his social media bio, and clicks links in emails that look like they’re from his favorite stores.

2. Risks and Consequences of Poor Email Practices

Credential Stuffing: If one website is hacked, and you reused your email/password, attackers can access your email and reset other accounts.

Data Breaches: Personal and sensitive information in emails (tax docs, bank statements, etc.) can be exposed.

Phishing: Scammers use fake emails to trick you into revealing passwords, payment info, or installing malware.

Identity Theft: If someone gets access to your email, they can impersonate you or take over other accounts.

Account Lockout: Your email is the gateway to most of your online life; if compromised, you could lose access to banking, shopping, and social accounts.

Example: A hacker guesses Mike’s weak password (since it leaked in a shopping site breach), resets his banking password using email recovery, and drains his account.

3. Practical Steps for Better Email Security

a) Use Strong, Unique Passwords

Use a long, complex password for your email—never reuse it elsewhere.

Consider a password manager to keep track of strong, unique passwords.

b) Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Turn on 2FA for your email. This adds a code (from your phone or app) after your password, making hacking much harder.

c) Watch Out for Phishing

Check the sender’s email address, not just their name.

Don’t click on links or download attachments unless you’re sure they’re legit—even if they seem urgent or important.

Hover over links before clicking to see where they really go.

d) Keep Your Email Private

Don’t post your primary email address publicly.

Use separate emails for important accounts (banking, work) and less important sign-ups (newsletters, stores).

e) Regularly Check Account Recovery Options

Make sure your backup email and phone number are up to date, and protected by strong passwords and 2FA as well.

f) Don’t Trust “Urgent” Requests

Never give personal information or send money in response to urgent email requests—even if it appears to come from someone you know.

g) Update and Secure Your Devices

Keep your operating system, browser, and antivirus software updated to protect against malware and phishing sites.

Log out of email on shared computers and avoid saving passwords on public devices.

4. Additional Tips for Safe Online Behavior

Be cautious with attachments: Even from people you know—confirm with them if the message seems unusual.

Review sent mail: Sometimes, hackers will use your account to spam your contacts. If you see emails you didn’t send, change your password and check your device for malware.

Watch for unfamiliar login alerts: If your provider notifies you of a login from a strange location or device, change your password immediately.

Back up important emails: Use secure cloud storage or encrypted backups for vital communications.

5. Easy Rules and Checklist

Golden Rules

One strong, unique password for your email.

2FA is a must.

Don’t click strange links or open odd attachments.

Never share your email password.

Keep recovery options up to date.

Quick Checklist

Strong, unique email password

Password manager in use

Two-factor authentication enabled

Devices and software up to date

Suspicious emails and links ignored or deleted

Public posting of primary email avoided

Backup/recovery info current

Real-World Scenario

Sarah receives an email claiming her tax return is ready, with a link to “download your document.” She clicks and logs in, but the site is fake—her login is stolen. The scammer uses her email account to reset her other logins and send phishing emails to her contacts.

How Sarah Could Have Been Safer:

Check the sender’s address and the link destination before clicking.

Enable 2FA on her email account.

Use a password manager and unique password.

Contact the IRS or her tax preparer directly instead of clicking a random link.
