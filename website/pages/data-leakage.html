Data Leakage Prevention

PROTECT YOUR COMPUTER

Understand how to prevent sensitive data from being exposed or compromised through various security measures.

How Everyday Users Should Think About Data Leakage

1. Common User Attitudes and Mistakes Around Data Leakage

Many users:

Share too much personal info online (social media, forums, public profiles) without considering who might see it.

Use the same email/password combos across different sites.

Trust every app or website with sensitive info (address, ID, payment info) without checking its reputation.

Ignore privacy settings or never review app permissions.

Assume “my info isn’t valuable” or “no one would want my data.”

Don’t update devices or apps, leaving security holes open.

Reuse, share, or store sensitive data in unsafe places (e.g., sending passwords over email or using unencrypted notes apps).

Example: Jess posts about her birthday, workplace, and vacation on Instagram. She reuses her email/password everywhere, and shares her home address in a public group to arrange a delivery.

2. Risks and Consequences of Poor Data Leakage Practices

Identity Theft: Criminals collect bits of personal info (birthdays, addresses, family names) and impersonate you or open accounts in your name.

Credential Stuffing: Using the same login on multiple sites means if one is breached, hackers can access others.

Phishing & Social Engineering: Attackers use leaked info to craft realistic scams (“Hi Jess, about your recent order…”).

Data Breaches: Sensitive files or info stored in unsecured locations can be exposed if a device, cloud account, or app is hacked.

Financial Loss: Leaked card/banking info leads to theft or fraudulent purchases.

Example: A scammer uses Jess’s publicly available info to reset her email password, then accesses her bank and social accounts.

3. Practical Steps to Improve Data Leakage Security

a) Be Cautious About Sharing Personal Info

Limit what you post on social media or public forums (e.g., don’t share your full birthday, address, or travel plans).

Use privacy settings to restrict who can see your posts and profile.

b) Use Strong, Unique Passwords

Never reuse passwords. Use a password manager to generate and store them.

Change passwords immediately if you hear of a breach.

c) Review App and Device Permissions

Check what data apps can access (location, contacts, files).

Deny permissions that aren’t necessary.

d) Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Turn on 2FA for email, banking, and social accounts.

e) Be Smart About Cloud Storage

Only store sensitive data (IDs, tax docs, health info) in secure, reputable cloud services—and enable 2FA.

Don’t keep sensitive info in plain text or unprotected notes apps.

f) Regularly Update Devices and Apps

Updates fix security holes that could leak data.

g) Think Before You Click or Share

Don’t enter info on unfamiliar sites or in response to suspicious emails/messages.

Hover over links and verify sender identity.

4. Additional Safe Online Habits

Monitor your digital footprint: Google yourself and review what’s public.

Use account recovery wisely: Make sure recovery email/phone is up to date and secure.

Watch for breach notifications: Use sites like HaveIBeenPwned.com to see if your info has been leaked.

Back up important data securely: Use encrypted drives or trusted cloud providers.

Log out of accounts on shared or public devices.

5. Easy-to-Remember Rules & Checklist

Golden Rules

If you wouldn’t put it on a billboard, don’t share it online.

Unique passwords for every account.

Check app and privacy settings often.

Update everything regularly.

Enable 2FA on important accounts.

Quick Data Leakage Checklist

Social media profiles set to private

No sensitive info in public posts

Password manager in use

Two-factor authentication enabled

App permissions reviewed

Cloud accounts secured

Devices/software updated

Real-World Scenario

Sam uses the same password for his email, photo storage, and shopping site. He posts his new address online to receive packages. After a breach at the shopping site, hackers use Sam’s leaked password to access his email, find sensitive info, and order products using his saved payment details.

How Sam Could Have Been Safer:

Use unique passwords with a manager.

Limit what he shares publicly.

Use 2FA on his email and storage accounts.

Regularly check for breaches and update passwords.
